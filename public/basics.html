Section 01

What You're Actually Trading

A futures contract is not a stock. When you buy one ES contract you are entering a legally binding agreement to exchange the value of the S&P 500 index at a future date. You are not buying a piece of a company. You are not investing. You are speculating on directional movement — and you are doing it with leverage.

That leverage is the point. One ES contract controls roughly $265,000 in notional value with an intraday margin of around $500–$1,000 depending on your broker. A 1-point move in ES is worth $50. The market moves 20–40 points on a normal day. Do the math on both sides of that.

This is not a warning. It is context. Leverage works the same way in both directions — and understanding exactly what you control is step one.

The Micro Solution

Every Signal Boss alert includes micro contract sizing. MES (Micro E-mini S&P) is 1/10th the size of ES — $5 per point instead of $50. You trade the same signal with $50–$100 of real risk instead of $500–$1,000. Micros exist specifically so you can learn and execute without betting the account on every trade.

Section 03

Your Stop Is Your Position Size

Most new traders decide how many contracts to trade, then figure out where to put the stop. This is backwards — and it's how you end up risking $800 when you meant to risk $100.

The correct workflow is: decide how much you're willing to lose on this trade first. Then let the stop location and tick value tell you how many contracts to trade. The signal gives you the stop. You bring the risk amount.

Risk amount → Stop location → Contract size.
In that order. Every time. Without exception.

Section 04

The Prop Account Reality

If you are trading a funded account — Apex, Topstep, FTMO, MyFundedFX, or any of the dozens of prop challenge providers — there is one number you need to understand before you place a single trade.

Your account size is not your trading capital. It is your breach threshold.

Account Reality Check

A "$50,000 funded account" typically has a maximum drawdown of 5–6%. That means you have $2,500–$3,000 before you breach and lose the account. That is your true working capital — whether it's a prop challenge or your own money with hard stops. Size every trade against the drawdown buffer, not the account balance.

A trader who sizes against a $50,000 account will blow through the drawdown limit in a handful of bad trades. A trader who sizes against $2,500 and treats that as their real capital survives long enough to let edge play out.

The Account Risk Calculator in your dashboard handles this. Enter your actual max drawdown — not your account size — as the "Loss to Ruin" number. Everything else flows from there.

Section 05

One Signal. One Decision.

When Signal Boss fires, the analysis is complete. The implied volatility data has been read. The expected move boundary has been identified. The entry, stop, and target have been calculated. Your job at that point is exactly one thing:

Take it or pass. That's the decision. Nothing else.

You do not move the stop after entry. You do not add contracts to a losing position. You do not override a take-profit target because the market "looks like it wants to go further." You do not hold past the signal exit time because you are up and feeling good.

Every one of those decisions introduces a variable that the backtest did not account for — which means every one of those decisions degrades the edge. The signal has a documented edge. You overriding the signal does not.

The Hard Part

Discipline is not a personality trait. It is a skill built through repetition. The traders who get consistent results are not smarter — they simply make fewer decisions per trade. Entry, stop, target. Set it. Leave it. That's the system.

Section 06

Why We Exit at End of Hour

Signal Boss signals are designed around intraday volatility expansion events. When conditions align — the expected move boundary, VWAP positioning, and momentum confirmation — a directional move is underway. That move has a natural lifecycle.

Most intraday momentum moves exhaust within 45–60 minutes of the setup forming. The first wave of institutional activity resolves. The options hedgers who drove the volatility signal have repositioned. The speculative follow-through completes.

Holding beyond that window introduces a different and largely unquantifiable risk: the macro, the Fed speaker, the news release, the random order flow of the afternoon. We capture the move that the signal identified. We do not try to capture everything after it.

Why This Matters for Your Account

End-of-hour exits mean defined exposure time. You know your maximum holding period before you enter. That predictability is part of what makes the strategy manageable for traders with jobs, families, and real lives — you are not glued to a screen all day waiting to see if a trade works out.
