Most traders are reading the market backwards.

Charts show you what already happened. Even a 1-minute bar is history. RSI, MACD, moving averages — every one of them is a derivative of price, telling you where it's been, not where it's going.

Here's what they're missing: We live in a derivatives-driven world. Futures and options are where institutions hedge real exposure — trillions in equities, commodities, bonds — and where speculators take the other side of that bet.

There are only two real participants moving price: the hedger and the speculator. When their positions fall out of balance, a move is coming. Every time. It's largely a function of the big players saying "you have to pay me more for that risk" — increased volatility is increased risk. Full stop.

Options are derivatives of those derivatives. And the options market is where that imbalance gets priced. When a hedger needs protection, they trade options combinations. This is why derivatives were created in the first place — to insure that unexpected, asymmetrical moves don't crush them on a future transaction. Hence the term: futures.

That demand for protection, coupled with speculator interest, reprices volatility. The expected move changes. Before the chart moves a single tick — the signal is already there. We're already on it.

Volume confirms it. But volume alone is incomplete and usually late to signal. The direction of that volume, weighted against price over time, tells you where the imbalance is being resolved. That's VWAP. That's the anchor institutions trade around, even when they'd never admit it.

Section 01

The Two Participants

Every price move in the futures market comes down to two forces.

Participant 01
The Hedger
Protecting real exposure. A pension fund holding $4 billion in equities doesn't want to sell during a volatility spike. They buy protection in the derivatives market. They're not guessing — they're paying to reduce risk on a move they believe is coming.
Participant 02
The Speculator
Takes the other side. No underlying position. They're there for the move — betting that the hedger's fear is overdone, or that the hedger isn't scared enough. Their willingness to take risk is what makes the market function.

When these two forces are in balance, price drifts. When they fall out of balance — when hedgers suddenly need more protection than speculators are willing to sell — price moves. Hard. Fast. And usually before any chart pattern gives you a signal.

That imbalance has a price. It's called volatility. And it changes before the candle does.

Real Example

April 2026. Tariff announcements hit overnight. Institutional desks holding equity index exposure don't wait for a chart pattern — they immediately buy put protection on ES and NQ futures. That demand reprices implied volatility upward before the open. The expected move widens. The signal is live. The chart hasn't moved yet.

Section 02

The Expected Move

Every morning, before markets open, the options market publishes something most futures traders have never looked at: the expected move.

It's not an opinion. It's not a forecast. It's the market's priced-in range for the session — derived from the cost of options protection at that moment. If institutions are paying up for hedges, the expected move widens. If they're calm, it contracts.

Section 03

VWAP: Where the Money Settles

Volume tells you how much traded. Price tells you where it traded. Neither alone tells you anything useful about institutional intent.

VWAP — Volume Weighted Average Price — tells you where the majority of capital transacted over a given period. It's the single benchmark every institutional desk uses to measure execution quality. A buy above VWAP is expensive. A sell below it is a concession.

Large players aren't ignoring this. They're evaluated against it.

Why It Matters

When price is above VWAP, institutions that need to sell are under pressure. When price is below, buyers have the upper hand. When price approaches VWAP with momentum from either direction — it's not retail traders driving it. It's the resolution of institutional imbalance.

Combined with the expected move, VWAP gives you something no lagging indicator can: context. Not where price was. Where the weight of money says it should be — right now.
